## Learning Objectives
- Communicate clearly and professionally with customers via telephone and email
- Deliver difficult news (cancellations, delays, refunds) with empathy
- Document all customer interactions in the PNR using remarks

## Overview
Technical GDS skills get you the booking right. Communication skills get you the customer for life. When a flight cancels at 11 PM and a passenger is panicking, your tone, clarity, and efficiency matter as much as your command of the GDS. Good communication also protects you legally — if you told the customer something and it's documented in the PNR, there's no dispute later.

## Key Concepts

### Concept 1: Telephone Etiquette
Answer promptly. Identify yourself: "Good evening, this is [Name] from [Company], how can I help?" Listen fully before acting. Repeat key information back (booking reference, flight details). End with a summary of what you'll do and when you'll follow up.

### Concept 2: Delivering Bad News
Flight cancelled? Say it directly, don't hedge: "Mr. Sharma, I'm afraid your AI302 flight on 20 June has been cancelled by the airline." Then immediately offer a solution: "The next available flight is AI320 at 10 AM on 21 June. Shall I rebook you on that?" Always give the customer agency — options, not just problems.

### Concept 3: Email Communication
For complex changes, always follow up phone calls with an email summarizing: what was discussed, what action was taken, any amounts collected or refunded, next steps. Keep it concise. Attach updated itineraries as PDF. This creates a paper trail.

### Concept 4: PNR Documentation
Every customer interaction should be reflected in the PNR. After a phone call: `RM CALLED CUSTOMER - EXPLAINED SCHEDULE CHANGE - OFFERED Rebooking ON AI320 21JUN 1000 - Customer confirmed`. This protects you and any colleague who picks up the PNR next.

### Concept 5: Managing Angry Customers
Acknowledge frustration first: "I understand this is frustrating, Mr. Patel. Let me help you resolve this." Never argue. Focus on solutions. If the situation exceeds your authority (refund above limit, exception to policy), escalate to a supervisor — but tell the customer you're escalating and give a timeline.

### Concept 6: Internal Communication
When handing off a PNR to a colleague (via queue or remark), document everything: `RM URGENT - CUSTOMER CALLED 3 TIMES - WANTS CONFIRMATION OF WHEELCHAIR - PHONE 9820098200`. Vague handoffs create重复 work and angry customers.

## Step-by-Step Guide
1. **Start calls professionally:** Identify yourself, confirm the customer's identity (booking reference + name).
2. **Listen first:** Let the customer explain the situation before taking action.
3. **Confirm details:** Repeat booking reference, flight, and request back to the customer.
4. **Take action:** Make the GDS change, explain what you're doing.
5. **Summarize:** "I've cancelled your original flight and booked you on AI320. Your new itinerary is..."
6. **Document:** Add `RM` with the call summary in the PNR.
7. **Follow up:** If you promised a callback or email, do it.

## Real-World Scenario
**Situation:** A customer calls in a panic — her mother was denied boarding at DEL because the airline couldn't find her booking. The record locator is XYZ789 and her mother's name is Sunita Sharma.

**Question:** How do you handle this?

**Answer:** First, stay calm and professional — the customer is stressed. Retrieve the PNR (`RTXYZ789`) and verify the booking exists and is confirmed (HK status). Check the name spelling against the passport via the DOCS SSR. Possible issue: name mismatch (e.g., "Sunita" in PNR vs. "Sunita Devi" on passport). Explain to the customer: "The booking is confirmed in our system. May I ask — does your mother's passport name match exactly what she gave us?" If there's a mismatch, this is a name correction issue (Module 8). If names match, escalate to the airline's station manager at DEL immediately. Document everything: `RM URGENT - MOTHER DENIED BOARDING AT DEL - NAME MISMATCH? - ESCALATED TO STATION MANAGER - REF XYZ789`. Keep the customer updated every 30 minutes.

## Common Mistakes
- **Mistake 1:** Not documenting phone conversations in the PNR
  - *Why it matters:* No paper trail — if the customer claims "you told me X," there's no evidence
  - *Correct approach:* Always add a `RM` after every significant customer interaction

- **Mistake 2:** Making promises you can't keep
  - *Why it matters:* "I'll put you on the next flight" when there is no next flight — customer frustration doubles
  - *Correct approach:* Only promise what you can deliver. Say "Let me check" before committing.

- **Mistake 3:** Giving inconsistent information across channels
  - *Why it matters:* Customer calls, gets answer A. Emails, gets answer B. Chaos ensues.
  - *Correct approach:* Document everything. If someone else handles the case, the PNR remark is the single source of truth.

## Key Glossary Terms
- **RM Documentation:** Recording customer interactions in the PNR for audit and continuity
- **Handoff:** Transferring a customer case to a colleague with full context
- **Escalation:** Moving a customer issue to a supervisor when it exceeds your authority
- **Paper Trail:** Written documentation of decisions, conversations, and actions taken

## Interview Tip
Say: "I understand that communication is as important as technical accuracy. I document every customer interaction in the PNR, send email summaries for complex changes, and always give customers clear options rather than just problems."
