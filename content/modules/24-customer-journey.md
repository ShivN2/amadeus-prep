## Learning Objectives
- Map the end-to-end customer journey from search to post-trip
- Identify GDS touchpoints at each stage of the journey
- Understand how each GDS action impacts customer experience

## Overview
Every flight booking is part of a larger customer journey: searching, booking, pre-trip preparation, travel day, and post-trip. As a GDS operator, you touch almost every stage. Understanding the full journey helps you anticipate needs — adding meals before the customer asks, checking visa requirements before ticketing, following up on schedule changes. This is what makes a GDS professional, not just a GDS operator.

## Key Concepts

### Concept 1: Search & Discovery
The journey starts when the customer decides to travel. They compare options on price, timing, and airline. Your GDS role: availability search (AN), fare display (FQD), schedule comparison. The quality of options you present shapes the entire trip.

### Concept 2: Booking & Payment
The customer selects a flight and pays. Your role: build the PNR (NM, SS, AP, TK, RF), price it (FXP), add form of payment (FP), and issue the ticket (TTP). Accuracy here is critical — name spelling, correct dates, right fare class.

### Concept 3: Pre-Trip Preparation
Between booking and travel: seat assignments (ST/SM), meal requests (SR VGML), visa checks (Timatic), frequent flyer (FFN), passport info (DOCS), special needs (wheelchair, infant). Proactive agents handle these at booking time, not the day before travel.

### Concept 4: Travel Day
Check-in, boarding, connections. The PNR and ticket you built are what the airline uses. If the name is wrong, check-in fails. If the connection violates MCT, the passenger is stuck. If the wheelchair SSR wasn't confirmed, there's no assistance. Everything you did (or didn't do) in the GDS shows up now.

### Concept 5: Disruption Handling
Flights get delayed, cancelled, or rerouted. Your role: monitor schedule changes (Queue 12), rebook passengers (SB, new availability search), process involuntary changes (no penalty), communicate proactively. This is where great agents shine — handling disruptions calmly and efficiently.

### Concept 6: Post-Trip
After travel: refund unused portions, process mileage claims, handle complaints, store customer preferences for next time. Good agencies build profiles — "Mr. Kumar always wants aisle, vegetarian, Star Alliance" — so the next booking is even smoother.

## Step-by-Step Guide
1. **Search:** `AN` for flights, `FQD` for fares — present 2-3 best options.
2. **Book:** Build PNR with all five mandatory elements + extras (FFN, meals, seats).
3. **Prepare:** Check Timatic, add DOCS, confirm SSRs, send itinerary to customer.
4. **Monitor:** Check Queue 12 for schedule changes. Proactively inform the customer.
5. **Disrupt:** If schedule changes, rebook immediately and communicate.
6. **Follow up:** Post-trip, handle any refunds/claims and note preferences.

## Real-World Scenario
**Situation:** Mrs. Rao books Delhi to Singapore return, 2 weeks out. She's vegetarian, has a 6-year-old child, and is a Star Alliance Silver member.

**Question:** How do you ensure a great end-to-end experience?

**Answer:** At booking: build the PNR, add her FFN (Star Alliance member → Air India or Singapore Airlines FFP), request VGML for her and CHML for the child, check Timatic (India to Singapore — no visa needed for stays under 30 days), assign seats together using the seat map, add DOCS with passport info. Send her a complete itinerary with booking reference and document checklist. Set a dated queue for 2 days before travel to verify no schedule changes. If any changes occur, call her immediately. Post-trip: verify miles were credited.

## Common Mistakes
- **Mistake 1:** Treating the booking as the end of the job
  - *Why it matters:* Customers need support through the entire journey, not just at booking
  - *Correct approach:* Add all SSRs at booking, monitor for changes, follow up proactively

- **Mistake 2:** Not monitoring schedule changes between booking and travel
  - *Why it matters:* Customer discovers at the airport that their flight time changed 3 hours earlier
  - *Correct approach:* Check Queue 12 regularly and contact affected passengers immediately

- **Mistake 3:** Not documenting customer preferences
  - *Why it matters:* Next booking, you ask the same questions again — unprofessional
  - *Correct approach:* Note preferences (seat, meal, airline, FFN) for repeat customers

## Key Glossary Terms
- **Customer Journey:** The complete experience from initial travel search through post-trip follow-up
- **Touchpoint:** Any moment where the customer interacts with a service (booking, check-in, boarding, etc.)
- **Proactive Service:** Anticipating customer needs before they ask (adding meals, checking visas, monitoring changes)
- **IRROPS (Irregular Operations):** Industry term for disruptions — delays, cancellations, diversions
- **Customer Profile:** A stored record of a passenger's preferences for faster future bookings

## Interview Tip
Say: "I think of GDS work as managing the entire customer journey, not just the booking. Proactive service — adding SSRs, checking documents, monitoring changes — is what differentiates quality service."
