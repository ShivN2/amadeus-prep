## Learning Objectives
- Understand how airlines set and adjust fares dynamically
- Explain the concept of yield management and booking classes
- Recognize how revenue management affects seat availability

## Overview
Ever wonder why the same seat on the same flight costs ₹4,000 today and ₹12,000 tomorrow? That's revenue management (also called yield management). Airlines use sophisticated algorithms to adjust prices based on demand, time to departure, competition, and booking patterns. Understanding this helps you explain pricing to customers and find better deals.

## Key Concepts

### Concept 1: Revenue Management
Airlines aim to maximize revenue per flight — not just fill every seat. They'd rather sell 150 seats at varying prices (some high, some low) than 180 seats all at the cheapest fare. Revenue management is the science of selling the right seat to the right customer at the right price at the right time.

### Concept 2: Booking Classes and Fare Buckets
Each booking class (Y, B, M, H, V, Q, etc.) represents a price point. The airline allocates a limited number of seats to each class. When V class (cheap) sells out, the next available is Q (slightly more expensive), then H, and so on. This is why fares increase as the flight fills up.

### Concept 3: Load Factor
The percentage of seats filled on a flight. A flight with 180 seats and 150 passengers has an 83% load factor. Airlines target high load factors but balance it with revenue — 100% load factor at the lowest fare isn't as profitable as 85% load factor with a mix of fare classes.

### Concept 4: Advance Purchase Effect
Fares are generally cheapest when booked far in advance. As the departure date approaches, cheap booking classes close and only expensive ones remain. This is by design — business travelers who book last-minute pay more than leisure travelers who book months ahead.

### Concept 5: Overbooking
Airlines intentionally sell more seats than the aircraft has because statistically, some passengers don't show up (no-shows). If everyone shows up, some passengers get "bumped" — usually with compensation. Revenue management calculates the optimal overbooking level.

### Concept 6: Seasonality and Demand
Fares spike during peak seasons (Diwali, summer holidays, Christmas) and drop during off-peak. Airlines adjust inventory by opening more cheap classes in low-demand periods and restricting them during high-demand periods. Understanding this helps you advise customers on timing.

## Step-by-Step Guide
1. When searching availability (AN), notice which booking classes are open — this reflects demand.
2. If cheap classes (V, Q) show 0 seats, the flight is filling up — fares will be higher.
3. For budget-conscious customers: book early, travel off-peak, and be flexible on dates.
4. For business customers: explain that last-minute fares are higher due to yield management.
5. Check fares for adjacent dates — sometimes one day difference saves significantly.
6. Understand that fare displays (FQD) show published fares, but availability determines which you can actually book.

## Real-World Scenario
**Situation:** A customer wants to fly DEL-BOM on 20 June. You check availability and only B class and above are open (V, Q, H are all at 0). The fare in B class is ₹12,000. The customer says "My friend booked the same flight for ₹4,000 last month."

**Question:** How do you explain this?

**Answer:** "Airlines use dynamic pricing. When your friend booked, cheaper fare classes (V class at ₹4,000) were still available. Since then, those have sold out, and the lowest available class now is B at ₹12,000. This is normal — fares increase as the flight fills up. If you're flexible, I can check adjacent dates where cheaper classes might still be open." Check `AN19JUNDELBOM` and `AN21JUNDELBOM` — the customer might save thousands by shifting one day.

## Common Mistakes
- **Mistake 1:** Promising a fare will stay available
  - *Why it matters:* Fares change in real-time — a fare available now may be gone in an hour
  - *Correct approach:* Say "This fare is available right now" and recommend booking promptly if the customer is ready

- **Mistake 2:** Not checking adjacent dates for better fares
  - *Why it matters:* The customer pays ₹12,000 when a one-day shift could save ₹8,000
  - *Correct approach:* Always offer date flexibility options for price-sensitive customers

- **Mistake 3:** Not understanding why the same flight has different prices for different passengers
  - *Why it matters:* Customer feels cheated and blames the agent
  - *Correct approach:* Explain booking classes and dynamic pricing — it's the airline's pricing model, not agent manipulation

## Key Glossary Terms
- **Revenue Management:** The practice of dynamically adjusting prices to maximize revenue per flight
- **Yield:** Revenue per passenger-kilometer — the unit airlines use to measure pricing efficiency
- **Load Factor:** The percentage of available seats that are sold on a flight
- **Overbooking:** Selling more seats than physically available, based on expected no-shows
- **Fare Bucket:** A group of seats allocated to a specific booking class and fare level

## Interview Tip
Say: "I understand yield management — airlines use booking classes to price dynamically based on demand. I can explain this to customers and help them find better value by being flexible on dates or booking early."
